# Victorian Inheritance-Control Plots Outside Jane Austen

*A short guide to novels in which wills, entails, settlements, reversions, and related devices are used to protect family property from weak, indebted, or morally unreliable heirs.*

## Anthony Trollope, *Doctor Thorne* — the cleanest spendthrift-heir case

Sir Roger Scatcherd is a self-made magnate who has acquired a fortune but knows that his son Louis Philippe Scatcherd is not fit to command it. Louis is young, dissipated, physically fragile, and already marked by drink and idleness. Sir Roger wants his son to enjoy the family’s new wealth and rank, but he also fears that immediate control of the fortune would destroy both the money and the heir.

His will therefore delays Louis’s full control. Dr. Thorne is made trustee, and Louis cannot freely command the bulk of the property until he reaches twenty-five. If Louis dies before that point without issue, the fortune is to pass elsewhere. The arrangement is explicitly protective: it is meant to keep a reckless son from squandering the family fortune before maturity can do its work.

The legal device succeeds, though not in the sentimental way Sir Roger hopes. Louis dies before he can take full possession, and the fortune ultimately passes to Mary Thorne, whose hidden family connection makes her the unexpected beneficiary. The plot is a near-perfect example of inheritance law as damage control: the father cannot make the heir prudent, but he can delay access to capital and provide an alternate line of succession.

## Anthony Trollope, *Mr. Scarborough’s Family* — inheritance law as anti-creditor warfare

Mr. Scarborough owns Tretton Park, a valuable entailed estate. His eldest son Mountjoy is the expected heir, but he is also a gambler, debtor, and wastrel. Mountjoy has borrowed heavily on post-obit terms: lenders give him money now because they expect to be repaid when he inherits. The family fortune is therefore in danger of passing through the heir straight into the hands of creditors.

Scarborough’s response is legally ingenious and morally ugly. He announces that Mountjoy, long treated as legitimate eldest son, was in fact born before his parents’ marriage and so cannot inherit. The younger son Augustus is thereby made heir. The maneuver protects the estate from Mountjoy’s creditors, but it also exposes the father’s willingness to use legitimacy, family shame, and technical law as weapons.

The novel is especially useful because it shows how inheritance expectations themselves become financial assets. A bad heir need not possess the estate in order to consume it; he can borrow against the future. Scarborough’s legal manipulation is an effort to break that connection between expected inheritance and present debt.

## Anthony Trollope, *Sir Harry Hotspur of Humblethwaite* — separating title from property

Sir Harry Hotspur’s son has died, so the baronetcy must pass to a distant male cousin, Captain George Hotspur. The estate, however, can be left to Sir Harry’s daughter Emily. The family’s dynastic instinct favors reuniting title and property by marrying Emily to George: the name, rank, and estate would then remain together.

The difficulty is that George is charming but corrupt: a gambler, debtor, liar, and scapegrace. Some characters imagine that marriage settlements and family discipline might make the arrangement safe. Sir Harry gradually sees that this is fantasy. To preserve the family name by handing Emily and the property to George would be to sacrifice the daughter to the title.

The novel dramatizes a painful shift from dynastic thinking to moral judgment. Sir Harry’s crucial act is not merely to dispose of property, but to decide that a legally proper male successor may nevertheless be an intolerable guardian of the family’s future.

## Other useful one-sentence examples

- **Anthony Trollope, *Ralph the Heir*:** an entailed estate must pass to a spendthrift nephew who can borrow against his future inheritance, showing how an entail can preserve a succession line while still exposing the estate to creditor pressure.
- **Anthony Trollope, *John Caldigate*:** a father responds to his son’s gambling debts and irresponsibility by effectively buying out the son’s expectations and sending him away to make a life outside the inheritance.
- **Anthony Trollope, *The Duke’s Children*:** the Duke of Omnium confronts Lord Silverbridge’s racing debts and tries to convert paternal payment into behavioral discipline.
- **Charles Dickens, *Our Mutual Friend*:** old Harmon’s will conditions John Harmon’s inheritance on marriage to Bella Wilfer, using testamentary power to govern the next generation’s conduct.
- **Charles Dickens, *Oliver Twist*:** Edward Leeford’s will makes Oliver’s inheritance depend on his remaining free from crime and dishonor, turning moral character into a legal condition of succession.
- **Emily Brontë, *Wuthering Heights*:** Edgar Linton tries to protect Cathy’s property from Heathcliff through trust-like arrangements, but the plot exposes how fragile such protections can be under coverture and coercive marriage.
- **Henry James, *Washington Square*:** Dr. Sloper threatens disinheritance to keep Catherine’s fortune from Morris Townsend, a fortune-hunter who has already wasted his own inheritance.
